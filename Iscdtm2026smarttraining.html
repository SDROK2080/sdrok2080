Day 1

Foundations & Digital-AI Literacy

Module 1 – Digital & AI Literacy · 08:45–14:30
Digital, Data & AI LiteracySchool of Data Nepal – free digital/AI literacy courses ↗
08:45–09:15  Opening Ceremony — welcome remarks by municipal officials and school heads, lamp lighting, introduction of facilitators and program goals.
▸09:15–09:45Ice-Breaking & Team Formation
Objective: Students feel comfortable with peers and facilitators, and are grouped into fixed teams they'll keep for the whole training.

Activity Steps

  1. Name-and-gesture circle: each student says their name with an action; the group repeats it back.
  2. "Find someone who…" mixer using cards with traits (loves football, has 3 siblings, wants to be an engineer, etc.).
  3. Count off into 5–6 fixed teams of 5–6 students; each team picks a name and a simple chant.
  4. Teams sit together for the rest of the training and will co-own the Day 7 community project.
Materials: Trait cards, team-name cards or markers, name tags.
Facilitator tip: Keep teams gender-balanced and mix schools/communities so students build new networks.
▸09:45–10:15Pre-Training Assessment
Objective: Capture each student's starting knowledge and attitudes across all 10 modules to measure growth later.

Content Areas Covered

  • Basic digital & AI awareness
  • Confidence in public speaking and teamwork
  • Self-rated problem-solving and career clarity
  • Financial habits and environmental awareness
  • Health, disaster preparedness, and civic knowledge

Format

  • 20–25 short multiple-choice and self-rating questions (paper or simple digital form).
  • Anonymous student ID codes used so pre/post scores can be matched without names.
Materials: Printed or digital questionnaire, pencils, ID code slips.
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:30Computer Basics & Safe Internet UseModule 1
Objective: Students operate a computer/smartphone confidently and browse the internet safely.

Key Content

  • Parts of a computer/smartphone: input, output, storage, and basic troubleshooting.
  • File and folder management: creating, renaming, saving, and organizing files.
  • Using a web browser: search engines, bookmarks, and evaluating website credibility.
  • Creating strong passwords and enabling two-factor authentication.
  • Recognizing phishing links, fake pop-ups, and unsafe downloads.

Activity Steps

  1. Guided walkthrough on shared/lab devices covering file management basics.
  2. Pair exercise: students create a folder structure for "My Schoolwork."
  3. Spot-the-phishing-email challenge using printed sample screenshots.
Materials: Computers/smartphones/tablets, sample phishing screenshots, projector.
Facilitator tip: Where device access is limited, run this as a rotating-station activity in small groups.
▸11:30–12:30Digital Citizenship & Online SafetyModule 1
Objective: Students understand their digital rights and responsibilities and can respond appropriately to online harm.

Key Content

  • What a "digital footprint" is and why it matters for future opportunities.
  • Recognizing and responding to cyberbullying, both as a target and a bystander.
  • Privacy settings on common social apps; what should never be shared publicly.
  • How and where to report online harm (school counselor, cyber bureau, platform reporting tools).

Activity Steps

  1. Split into small groups; assign each a scenario card (e.g., a friend is being bullied online, a stranger asks for a photo).
  2. Groups role-play the scenario twice: once showing a poor response, once a safe response.
  3. Whole-group debrief: list the reporting channels available locally.
Materials: Scenario cards, flip chart for reporting-channel list.
12:30–13:00  Lunch Break
▸13:00–14:00AI Tools & Responsible AI UseModule 1
Objective: Students understand what AI tools can and cannot do, and how to use them honestly in schoolwork.

Key Content

  • What AI is in simple terms: pattern-based prediction, not "thinking" like a human.
  • Common AI tools students may encounter: chatbots, translation apps, image generators, homework helpers.
  • Benefits: faster research, language help, idea generation.
  • Limits: AI can be wrong, biased, or outdated; it should never replace a student's own understanding.
  • Academic honesty: when AI help is acceptable versus when it counts as cheating; how to acknowledge AI assistance.

Activity Steps

  1. Live demo: facilitator asks an AI chatbot a question in front of the class and shows a mistake it makes.
  2. Class discussion: how would you check if an AI's answer is correct?
  3. Small groups draft 3 personal "rules" for responsible AI use in school; share out.
Materials: Projector/internet access for live demo, chart paper for group rules.
Facilitator tip: If internet access is unavailable, use printed screenshots of a real AI conversation, including an error, to run the same discussion.
▸14:00–14:30Daily Reflection & Journaling
Objective: Reinforce learning and give facilitators a continuous-assessment record.

Prompt

  • One thing I learned today…
  • One question I still have…
  • One way I will use this at home or school…
Materials: Reflection journal or worksheet per student.
Day 2

Communication & Leadership

Module 2 – Communication & Leadership · 08:45–14:30
Communication & LeadershipNepal Leadership Academy – LEAD framework & youth leadership courses ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — voice warm-up (tongue twisters, mirror-speaking pairs).
▸09:00–10:15Public Speaking FundamentalsModule 2
Objective: Students structure and deliver a short, confident speech.

Key Content

  • Basic speech structure: opening hook, 2–3 main points, clear closing.
  • Managing stage fear: breathing techniques, practicing out loud, focusing on friendly faces.
  • Voice control: pace, volume, and pausing for emphasis.
  • Body language: posture, eye contact, purposeful gestures.

Activity Steps

  1. Facilitator models a 1-minute speech using the structure above.
  2. Students draft a 1-minute speech on a simple topic (e.g., "My favorite place in my community").
  3. Each student delivers it to a partner, then a few volunteers present to the full group.
Materials: Topic-prompt cards, timer.
Facilitator tip: Praise effort and structure first; save delivery polish for the Day 2 afternoon workshop.
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:45Teamwork & Group DynamicsModule 2
Objective: Students recognize productive team roles and behaviors.

Key Content

  • Stages of team development: forming, storming, norming, performing.
  • Common team roles: organizer, timekeeper, note-taker, presenter, idea-generator.
  • Communication habits that help teams: listening fully, disagreeing respectfully, sharing credit.

Activity Steps

  1. Human-knot activity: teams tangle hands and must untangle without letting go, practicing patient communication.
  2. Tower-building challenge: teams build the tallest freestanding tower from limited materials in 10 minutes.
  3. Debrief: which roles emerged naturally? What helped or hurt teamwork?
Materials: Straws/newspaper/tape for tower challenge, open space for human knot.
11:45–12:30  Lunch Break
▸12:30–13:30Leadership Styles & QualitiesModule 2
Objective: Students identify leadership traits they want to build in themselves.

Key Content

  • Leadership styles in simple terms: directive (gives clear instructions), participative (invites input), supportive (puts the team's needs first).
  • Core qualities: integrity, empathy, decisiveness, accountability.
  • Leadership is an action, not a title — anyone can lead in a moment.

Activity Steps

  1. Short case studies of local or national youth leaders/change-makers are read aloud or handed out.
  2. Groups identify which style and qualities the case study shows.
  3. Each student writes one leadership quality they will practice this month.
Materials: Printed case-study handouts.
▸13:30–14:15Presentation Skills WorkshopModule 2
Objective: Students plan and deliver a short group presentation with visual support.

Key Content

  • Keeping visual aids simple: one idea per slide/poster, large clear text.
  • Dividing speaking parts fairly within a group.
  • Rehearsing out loud at least once before presenting.

Activity Steps

  1. Teams receive an assigned topic and 20 minutes to prepare a 2-minute presentation with a simple poster.
  2. Each team presents; peers score using a short rubric (clarity, teamwork, confidence).
  3. Facilitator gives one strength and one growth point per team.
Materials: Chart paper, markers, peer-feedback rubric slips.
14:15–14:30  Daily Reflection — journal entry and quick group share-out on confidence gained.
Day 3

Critical Thinking & Problem Solving

Module 3 – Critical Thinking & Problem Solving · 08:45–14:30
Critical Thinking & PBLEdutopia – Project-Based Learning resource hub ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — a lateral-thinking riddle solved in pairs.
▸09:00–10:15Logical Reasoning & Analytical ThinkingModule 3
Objective: Students practice structured reasoning and evaluating evidence.

Key Content

  • Deductive reasoning (general rule to specific case) versus inductive reasoning (specific cases to general pattern).
  • Spotting common reasoning traps: jumping to conclusions, confusing correlation with causation.
  • Basic evidence evaluation: is the source reliable? Is the sample size reasonable?

Activity Steps

  1. Warm-up puzzles solved individually, then discussed as a class.
  2. Small groups analyze 2–3 short "claim + evidence" cards and decide if the reasoning holds up.
  3. Groups share their verdicts and reasoning with the class.
Materials: Puzzle handouts, claim-evidence cards.
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:45Introduction to Project-Based LearningModule 3
Objective: Students understand how to turn a real problem into a structured project.

Key Content

  • What makes a good "driving question" — specific, locally relevant, and answerable through action.
  • The research → design → act cycle used in project-based learning.
  • Example: a school's water wastage problem, from noticing it to designing a fix.

Activity Steps

  1. Facilitator walks through the example project step by step.
  2. Groups brainstorm 3 possible driving questions from their own school/community.
  3. Groups vote on their strongest question to carry forward toward Day 7.
Materials: Flip chart, sticky notes for brainstorming.
11:45–12:30  Lunch Break
▸12:30–13:45Innovation & Design ThinkingModule 3
Objective: Students apply a simple design-thinking process to a real problem.

Key Content

  • The five stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, test.
  • "Empathize" means talking to the people actually affected by the problem before proposing a fix.
  • A prototype can be as simple as a sketch or a paper model — it doesn't need to be finished or fancy.

Activity Steps

  1. Groups pick a real school or community problem and interview 1–2 classmates about it ("empathize").
  2. Groups write a one-sentence problem definition.
  3. Groups sketch a simple, low-cost solution on a design-thinking worksheet.
Materials: Design-thinking worksheets, pencils, scrap paper for prototyping.
▸13:45–14:15Mini Case-Study ChallengeModule 3
Objective: Apply reasoning and design skills under light time pressure.

Activity Steps

  1. Each group receives a short scenario (e.g., "Your school canteen is wasting a lot of food — what would you do in the next 2 weeks?").
  2. Groups have 15 minutes to propose a solution using what they learned today.
  3. Each group presents its solution in 2 minutes; facilitator highlights strong reasoning.
Materials: Scenario cards, timer.
14:15–14:30  Daily Reflection — journal entry on a problem-solving approach to reuse.
Day 4

Career Guidance & Financial Literacy

Module 4 – Career Guidance & Module 5 – Financial Literacy · 08:45–14:30
Career GuidanceCTEVT Nepal – technical & vocational education pathways ↗ Financial LiteracyNepal Rastra Bank – Financial Literacy Framework & resources ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — pair-share: "What did you want to be at age 8?"
▸09:00–10:00Career Planning & Self-AssessmentModule 4
Objective: Students connect their own interests and strengths to realistic career clusters.

Key Content

  • Career clusters relevant locally: health, education, agriculture, technology, trades/vocational, business.
  • Interests vs. skills vs. values, and why all three matter in choosing a path.
  • Short-, medium-, and long-term career goals.

Activity Steps

  1. Students complete a simple interests-and-strengths worksheet.
  2. Students match their top interests to 2–3 possible career clusters.
  3. Pair-share: students explain their match to a partner.
Materials: Self-assessment worksheets.
▸10:00–10:15Entrepreneurship & Vocational OpportunitiesModule 4
Objective: Students hear a real, local example of an entrepreneurial or vocational path.

Key Content

  • Types of local livelihoods: small business, agriculture-based enterprise, skilled trades, freelance/digital work.
  • What it actually takes to start small: initial idea, basic costs, first customers.
Materials: None — guest speaker session.
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:15Entrepreneurship Q&A and Idea PitchModule 4
Objective: Students generate and refine a simple business or service idea.

Key Content

  • Simplified idea framework: problem → who has it → your solution → what it costs to start.

Activity Steps

  1. Students ask the guest speaker questions about starting out, risk, and failure.
  2. In pairs, students sketch one small idea using the 4-part framework above.
  3. A few pairs share their idea informally with the group.
Materials: Simple idea-framework worksheet.
11:15–12:00  Lunch Break
▸12:00–13:00Budgeting & SavingModule 5
Objective: Students build a simple personal budget and understand saving as a habit.

Key Content

  • Needs versus wants, and why prioritizing needs matters.
  • Basic budgeting formula: income − expenses = savings.
  • Setting a small, specific savings goal with a timeline.

Activity Steps

  1. Students receive a sample monthly allowance amount and a list of typical expenses.
  2. Individually, students allocate the amount across needs, wants, and savings on a worksheet.
  3. Volunteers share how they prioritized and why.
Materials: Budgeting worksheets, pencils.
▸13:00–14:15Banking & Digital PaymentsModule 5
Objective: Students understand how to use banking and digital payment tools safely.

Key Content

  • What a savings account is and basic steps to open one.
  • How mobile/digital payment apps work in simple terms.
  • Safety rules: never share PIN/OTP, verify before sending money, check transaction confirmations.

Activity Steps

  1. Guided walkthrough (in person or via a local bank representative) of opening a savings account.
  2. Demonstration of a mobile payment app's safety features.
  3. Quick quiz: "safe or unsafe?" scenario cards on digital payments.
Materials: Sample account-opening form, projector for app demo, scenario cards.
14:15–14:30  Daily Reflection — journal entry on one career step and one saving habit to start this month.
Day 5

Environment & Well-being

Module 6 – Environmental & Climate Education & Module 7 – Health & Well-being · 08:45–14:30
Environment & ClimateMinistry of Forests & Environment, Nepal ↗ Health & Well-beingMinistry of Health & Population – Adolescent Health Strategy ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — outdoor stretch and breathing activity.
▸09:00–10:00Waste Management & the 3RsModule 6
Objective: Students correctly sort waste and understand reduce-reuse-recycle in daily life.

Key Content

  • Reduce, reuse, recycle: what each means with local, everyday examples.
  • Organic versus inorganic waste, and basic composting.
  • Where the school/community's waste currently goes, and what could improve.

Activity Steps

  1. Facilitator sets up sample waste items (clean, safe examples) at a sorting station.
  2. Students sort items into reduce/reuse/recycle/compost bins.
  3. Group discussion: one change the school could make this term.
Materials: Sample waste items, labeled bins/boxes.
▸10:00–10:15Biodiversity & Climate Adaptation (Intro)Module 6
Objective: Students connect global climate concepts to local, visible changes.

Key Content

  • What biodiversity means and why it matters for food, water, and health.
  • Visible signs of climate change in the local area: changing rainfall patterns, temperature shifts, crop impacts.
  • Basic adaptation ideas: water conservation, tree planting, resilient crops.
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:30Nature Walk / Field ObservationModule 6
Objective: Students observe real biodiversity and environmental conditions firsthand.

Activity Steps

  1. Guided walk around the school or nearby community area in small groups.
  2. Students use an observation checklist: plants seen, water sources, signs of pollution or erosion.
  3. Groups discuss one environmental concern they noticed and one possible action.
Materials: Observation checklist sheets, pencils.
Facilitator tip: If a field walk isn't possible, use printed local photos and satellite images instead.
11:30–12:15  Lunch Break
▸12:15–13:15Mental Health & Stress ManagementModule 7
Objective: Students recognize stress and practice a healthy coping technique.

Key Content

  • Common signs of stress in students: trouble sleeping, irritability, difficulty concentrating.
  • Healthy coping strategies: talking to a trusted adult, physical activity, breathing exercises, journaling.
  • When and how to seek help — school counselor, family member, health worker.

Activity Steps

  1. Open, age-appropriate discussion on what causes stress for students.
  2. Guided breathing/mindfulness exercise (4-count inhale, hold, exhale).
  3. Students write down one person they can talk to if they feel overwhelmed.
Materials: Quiet space, optional mat/cushions.
▸13:15–14:15Nutrition & Reproductive HealthModule 7
Objective: Students receive accurate, age-appropriate information on nutrition and reproductive health.

Key Content

  • Balanced diet basics and common local nutrition gaps.
  • Physical and emotional changes during puberty.
  • Personal hygiene practices.
  • Separating common myths from facts in a supportive, non-judgmental way.

Activity Steps

  1. Session led by a qualified health worker or trained facilitator, with genders grouped as locally appropriate.
  2. Anonymous question-box: students submit questions in writing for the health worker to answer.
  3. Closing reminder on where to get further confidential support.
Materials: Question box, slips of paper, age-appropriate health materials/posters.
Facilitator tip: Confirm in advance that the health worker is trained in adolescent-appropriate communication.
14:15–14:30  Daily Reflection — journal entry on one environmental action and one well-being habit to adopt.
Day 6

Safety & Citizenship

Module 8 – Disaster Risk Reduction & Module 9 – Civic Education · 08:45–14:30
Disaster Risk ReductionNDRRMA – National Disaster Risk Reduction & Management Authority ↗ Civic EducationNepal Law Commission – Constitution of Nepal (full text) ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — practicing "duck-cover-hold" positioning as a warm-up.
▸09:00–10:15Earthquake & Fire PreparednessModule 8
Objective: Students know and can perform correct earthquake and fire response actions.

Key Content

  • "Drop, Cover, Hold" technique for earthquakes.
  • Identifying safe zones and hazards inside a classroom (heavy furniture, glass, exits).
  • School evacuation routes and assembly points.
  • Basic fire response: stop-drop-roll, using an extinguisher (PASS method), when to evacuate versus fight a small fire.

Activity Steps

  1. Facilitator from the local disaster management office demonstrates "Drop, Cover, Hold."
  2. Full live earthquake evacuation drill to the assembly point, timed and debriefed.
  3. Fire safety demonstration, including extinguisher use if equipment is available.
Materials: Whistle, evacuation route map, fire extinguisher (demo unit if available).
10:15–10:30  Tea Break
▸10:30–11:45Flood Preparedness & First AidModule 8
Objective: Students recognize flood risk signs and perform basic first aid.

Key Content

  • Local flood warning signs and safe versus unsafe response actions (never walk/drive through moving water).
  • Basic first aid: cleaning and bandaging a wound, recovery position, when to call for emergency help.
  • Introductory CPR awareness (recognizing an emergency and the importance of calling for help immediately).

Activity Steps

  1. Short talk on local flood risk areas and safe evacuation behavior.
  2. Hands-on first-aid stations: bandaging practice, recovery position practice, in small rotating groups.
  3. Facilitator-led CPR awareness demonstration on a mannequin, if available.
Materials: First-aid kit, bandages, CPR mannequin (if available), local flood-risk map.
11:45–12:30  Lunch Break
▸12:30–13:30Constitution & Local GovernanceModule 9
Objective: Students understand key features of the constitution and their local government structure.

Key Content

  • Core principles of the constitution: fundamental rights, federal structure, rule of law.
  • Three tiers of government and how they relate: federal, provincial, and local.
  • Structure of the local municipality: mayor/chairperson, ward offices, municipal assembly, and their basic functions.
  • How a student or citizen can raise a concern with the local government.

Activity Steps

  1. Short interactive lecture with a simple diagram of government tiers.
  2. Quiz game (team-based buzzer or hands-up format) on key facts.
  3. Discussion: one local issue students could raise with their ward office.
Materials: Government structure diagram/poster, quiz questions.
▸13:30–14:15Citizenship & the SDGsModule 9
Objective: Students connect the Sustainable Development Goals to real local issues.

Key Content

  • Overview of the 17 SDGs, grouped simply (people, planet, prosperity, peace, partnership).
  • What "active citizenship" looks like at a student's scale: volunteering, respecting rules, participating in local decisions.

Activity Steps

  1. Groups receive SDG cards and match them to local issues they've observed (e.g., waste → SDG 11/12, health → SDG 3).
  2. Each group shares one match and one action a student could take toward it.
Materials: SDG cards/poster.
14:15–14:30  Daily Reflection — journal entry on one civic action and one safety step to share with family.
Day 7

Community Project & Closing

Module 10 – Community Project, Post-Training Assessment & Closing · 08:45–14:30
Community Project & SDGsNational Planning Commission, Nepal – SDG localization ↗
08:45–09:00  Attendance & Energizer — teams reconvene with their Day 1 chant.
▸09:00–09:45Community Project PlanningModule 10
Objective: Each fixed team finalizes a real, locally relevant issue and a simple action plan.

Key Content

  • Criteria for a good project topic: locally relevant, small enough to act on in one day, safe to investigate.
  • Structure of a one-page action plan: issue, why it matters, what we'll do, who does what.

Activity Steps

  1. Teams review the driving questions drafted back on Day 3 and pick their final topic.
  2. Teams complete a one-page action plan template.
  3. Facilitator quickly reviews each plan for feasibility and safety before fieldwork.
Materials: Action-plan template, pens.
▸09:45–11:00Community Survey / Field VisitModule 10
Objective: Teams gather real information or opinions relevant to their chosen issue.

Key Content

  • Writing 3–5 clear, simple survey/interview questions.
  • Respectful data-collection etiquette: introducing yourself, asking permission, thanking participants.

Activity Steps

  1. Teams finalize their short question list with facilitator sign-off.
  2. Teams go into the community (or interview visiting community members/school staff) to collect responses.
  3. Teams regroup and compile their raw findings.
Materials: Survey sheets, clipboards, pens.
Facilitator tip: Keep fieldwork within a supervised, pre-agreed radius of the venue for student safety.
11:00–11:15  Tea Break
▸11:15–12:15Project Development & Report WritingModule 10
Objective: Teams turn their findings into a short, clear report and presentation.

Key Content

  • Simple report structure: the issue, what we found, what we recommend.
  • Using one chart or visual to make findings easier to understand.

Activity Steps

  1. Teams summarize survey findings into 2–3 key points.
  2. Teams build a short poster/report and assign speaking parts for the presentation.
  3. Quick rehearsal within the team.
Materials: Chart paper, markers, findings notes.
12:15–13:00  Lunch Break
▸13:00–13:45Community Project PresentationsModule 10
Objective: Teams present their project to a panel and receive structured feedback.

Evaluation Rubric

  • Relevance of the issue to the community
  • Quality and honesty of the data collection
  • Clarity of the presentation
  • Practicality of the recommendation

Activity Steps

  1. Each team presents for 5 minutes to a panel of trainers, teachers, and municipal representatives.
  2. Panel asks 1–2 questions per team.
  3. Panel scores using the rubric above; brief public feedback given to each team.
Materials: Scoring rubric sheets for panelists.
▸13:45–14:05Post-Training Assessment
Objective: Measure knowledge and skill gained across all 10 modules against the Day 1 baseline.
  • Same question format and content areas as the pre-training assessment.
  • Matched using the same anonymous student ID codes from Day 1.
Materials: Post-assessment questionnaire, ID code slips.
▸14:05–14:30Closing Ceremony & Certificate Distribution
Objective: Formally close the training and recognize student achievement.
  • Closing remarks from municipal officials and lead facilitator.
  • Quick feedback collection from students on the training experience.
  • Group photo.
  • Certificates of Completion distributed to students meeting attendance and performance criteria.
Materials: Certificates, feedback forms, camera.
