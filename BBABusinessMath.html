Unit 1 · Straight Lines & Functions
Linear Functions and Their Applications in Market Analysis
+

Almost every business question — "how much will we sell at this price?", "when do we break even?" — starts life as a straight line on a graph.

Key ideas
  • Slope-intercept form: y = mx + c, where m is the slope (rate of change) and c is the vertical intercept.
  • Demand function: price falls as quantity rises, so the demand line has a negative slope: P = a − bQ.
  • Supply function: producers offer more at higher prices, so the supply line has a positive slope: P = c + dQ.
  • Cost, revenue and elasticity: C(Q) = fixed cost + variable cost × Q; R(Q) = price × Q; elasticity measures how strongly quantity reacts to a change in price, income, or another good's price.
  • Budget & cost constraints are just straight lines drawn as limits rather than relationships.
  • Method of least squares: when real data doesn't sit perfectly on a line, this method finds the straight line that fits the scattered points best.
Core formulas
slope  m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁)
line through a point  y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
linear demand  P = a − bQ  ·  linear supply  P = c + dQ
equilibrium  set demand = supply, solve for Q, then P Lab work in this unit is done in Excel — plotting lines and solving for intersections directly from data.
Worked example
Market equilibrium Demand: P = 100 − 2Q  ·  Supply: P = 20 + 3Q
Set equal: 100 − 2Q = 20 + 3Q → 80 = 5Q → Q = 16 units
Substitute back: P = 100 − 2(16) = Rs 68 — the equilibrium price and quantity where the market clears.

Unit 2 · Simultaneous Equations
Equilibrium, Break-Even and the IS-LM Model
+

Two lines crossing on a graph is a market clearing; a business's cost line crossing its revenue line is the day it stops losing money.

Key ideas
  • Solving simultaneous linear equations by substitution or elimination — the algebra behind every equilibrium diagram.
  • Equilibrium and break-even: equilibrium is where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied; break-even is where total revenue equals total cost.
  • Consumer and producer surplus (introduced here, developed fully with integration in Unit 7) — the extra value buyers and sellers gain from trading at the equilibrium price.
  • The IS-LM model: a macroeconomic system of two simultaneous relationships — the goods market (IS) and the money market (LM) — solved together to find the interest rate and income level that balance both.
Core formulas
Break-even point (units)  = Fixed Cost ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit)
Break-even point (revenue)  = Break-even units × Selling Price
Contribution margin  = Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit
Worked example
Break-even analysis Fixed cost = Rs 1,00,000  ·  Selling price = Rs 50/unit  ·  Variable cost = Rs 30/unit
Break-even units = 1,00,000 ÷ (50 − 30) = 1,00,000 ÷ 20 = 5,000 units
Break-even revenue = 5,000 × 50 = Rs 2,50,000 — below this, the firm makes a loss; above it, a profit.

Unit 3 · Quadratic Equations
Graphs of Quadratic Functions & Applications to Economics
+

Profit doesn't climb forever as you sell more — a parabola shows exactly where it turns around and starts falling.

Key ideas
  • General quadratic: y = ax² + bx + c, whose graph is a parabola — opening upward if a > 0, downward if a < 0.
  • Solving quadratics: by factoring or by the quadratic formula.
  • Revenue as a quadratic: if price falls linearly as quantity rises (P = a − bQ), then Revenue = P×Q becomes a quadratic in Q — automatically producing a "hill-shaped" revenue curve.
  • The vertex of the parabola gives the maximum revenue/profit (downward-opening curve) or the minimum cost (upward-opening curve).
Core formulas
Quadratic formula  x = [ −b ± √(b² − 4ac) ] / 2a
Vertex (turning point)  x = −b / 2a
Revenue as quadratic  R(Q) = P(Q) × Q
Worked example
Revenue maximisation Revenue function: R(Q) = −2Q² + 400Q
Vertex at Q = −400 / (2×−2) = 100 units
Maximum revenue: R(100) = −2(100)² + 400(100) = Rs 20,000

Unit 4 · Non-Linear Functions & Numerical Methods
Cubic, Exponential, Log & Hyperbolic Curves; Bisection & Newton-Raphson
+

Not every business curve is a straight line or a parabola — and when the equation gets too messy to solve by hand, we let a simple, repeatable procedure close in on the answer instead.

Key ideas
  • Cubic and polynomial functions — used where cost or output curves have more than one bend.
  • Exponential functions — model unrestricted growth or decay (population, compound processes).
  • Logarithmic functions — the inverse of exponentials, used to solve for "how long" or "what rate" questions.
  • Hyperbolic functions of the form a /(bx + c) — common in average-cost curves that fall sharply then flatten out.
  • Bisection method: bracket a root between two points of opposite sign, then repeatedly halve the interval — slow, but never fails.
  • Newton-Raphson method: use the tangent line at a guess to jump straight toward the root — fast, but needs the derivative and a reasonable starting guess.
Core formulas
Bisection  midpoint c = (a + b)/2 — replace whichever endpoint has the same sign as f(c)
Newton-Raphson  xₙ₊₁ = xₙ − f(xₙ) / f′(xₙ)
Worked example
Root of f(x) = x² − 2 (i.e. √2) Bisection on [1, 2]: midpoint 1.5 → root in [1, 1.5]; midpoint 1.25 → root in [1.25, 1.5]; midpoint 1.375 → narrowing toward 1.414 — accurate, but needs many steps.
Newton-Raphson from x₀ = 1.5: x₁ = 1.5 − (0.25/3) = 1.41667; x₂ = 1.41667 − (0.00694/2.83333) ≈ 1.41421 — matches √2 after just two steps.

Unit 5 · Financial Mathematics
Interest, Annuities, Depreciation, NPV/IRR & Bonds
+

Whether it's a fixed deposit, a business loan or a government bond, all of finance rests on one idea: a rupee today is worth more than a rupee tomorrow.

Key ideas
  • Sequences & series (arithmetic and geometric) underpin every annuity and loan-repayment formula.
  • Simple vs. compound interest — simple interest grows in a straight line; compound interest grows exponentially because interest itself earns interest.
  • Nominal vs. effective (APR) rates — the true annual cost once compounding frequency is accounted for.
  • Depreciation — spreading an asset's fall in value over its useful life.
  • Net Present Value (NPV) and Internal Rate of Return (IRR) — the two core tools for deciding whether an investment is worthwhile.
  • Annuities, debt repayment & sinking funds — equal periodic payments used to save toward, or pay off, a lump sum.
  • Bond prices move opposite to interest rates — when rates rise, existing bond prices fall, and vice versa.
Core formulas
Compound value  FV = PV (1 + i)ⁿ  ·  Present value  PV = FV (1 + i)⁻ⁿ
PV of an ordinary annuity  PV = PMT × [ 1 − (1+i)⁻ⁿ ] / i
NPV  = Σ [ CFₜ / (1+r)ᵗ ] − Initial outlay  ·  IRR  = the rate r that makes NPV = 0
Worked example
Present value of an annuity Rs 10,000 received at the end of each year for 5 years, at 10% interest.
PV = 10,000 × [1 − (1.10)⁻⁵] / 0.10 = 10,000 × 3.7908 = Rs 37,908 — that lump sum today is equivalent to the whole 5-year stream.

Unit 6 · Differentiation & Applications
Marginal Analysis, Optimisation & Elasticity
+

"Marginal" is just calculus in a business suit — the derivative tells you exactly what happens if you make one more unit.

Key ideas
  • The derivative as a slope: the rate at which a function is changing at a point.
  • Rules of differentiation — power, sum, product, quotient and chain rules.
  • Marginal cost, marginal revenue, marginal profit — the derivatives of the cost, revenue and profit functions.
  • Optimisation: a function is maximised or minimised where its derivative equals zero; the second derivative tells you which.
  • Economic optimum: profit is maximised exactly where Marginal Revenue = Marginal Cost.
  • Elasticity via the derivative — a precise, point-by-point version of "how responsive is demand to price".
Core formulas
Marginal cost  MC(Q) = C′(Q)  ·  Marginal revenue  MR(Q) = R′(Q)
Profit maximised where  MR(Q) = MC(Q)  (equivalently, π′(Q) = 0)
Point elasticity of demand  E = (P/Q) × (dQ/dP)
Worked example
Marginal cost & profit maximisation If C(Q) = 500 + 20Q + 0.1Q², then MC(Q) = 20 + 0.2Q. At Q = 100, MC = Rs 40 — the 101st unit adds about Rs 40 to cost.

If R(Q) = 200Q − Q² and C(Q) = 1000 + 20Q, profit is π(Q) = 180Q − Q² − 1000. Setting MR = MC: 200 − 2Q = 20 gives Q = 90 units, with maximum profit π(90) = Rs 7,100.

Unit 7 · Integration & Applications
Total Functions, Consumer & Producer Surplus, Area Under a Curve
+

If differentiation zooms in on a rate of change, integration zooms back out — turning marginal cost into total cost, and a demand curve into the exact rupee value of a good deal for the buyer.

Key ideas
  • Integration as the reverse of differentiation — standard forms, substitution, and integration by parts.
  • Definite integrals as area: the integral of a curve between two points equals the area under it.
  • Total cost/revenue from marginal functions: integrating MC(Q) recovers C(Q) (plus the fixed cost as the constant of integration); the same works for revenue.
  • Consumer surplus: the area between the demand curve and the horizontal equilibrium-price line — the extra value buyers would have paid but didn't have to.
  • Producer surplus: the area between the equilibrium-price line and the supply curve — the extra revenue sellers receive above their minimum acceptable price.
Core formulas
Consumer surplus  CS = ∫₀^Qe D(Q) dQ − Pe·Qe
Producer surplus  PS = Pe·Qe − ∫₀^Qe S(Q) dQ
Worked example
Consumer & producer surplus Demand: P = 50 − Q  ·  Supply: P = ⅔Q  ·  Equilibrium at Qe = 30, Pe = 20
CS = ∫₀³⁰(50−Q)dQ − 20(30) = 1050 − 600 = Rs 450
PS = 20(30) − ∫₀³⁰ ⅔Q dQ = 600 − 300 = Rs 300  →  total social gain = Rs 750

Unit 8 · Linear Inequalities & Linear Programming
Graphical Method, Simplex Method & Duality
+

Every manager juggling limited labour, machine time and money is — often without realising it — solving a linear programming problem.

Key ideas
  • Linear inequalities in two variables define a feasible region on a graph — everything a firm is allowed to produce given its constraints.
  • Formulating an LP: choose decision variables, write the objective function (what to maximise or minimise), list the constraints, and add non-negativity conditions.
  • Graphical method: for two variables, plot the feasible region and test its corner points — the optimum always sits at a corner.
  • Simplex method: for more than two variables, convert inequalities to equations using slack variables and pivot systematically through a tableau until no further improvement is possible.
  • Duality: every LP has a mirror-image "dual" problem — a maximisation problem's dual is a minimisation problem, and its solution reveals the shadow price of each resource.
Core formulas
Standard form  maximise Z = c₁x₁ + c₂x₂ + …  subject to a₁₁x₁+a₁₂x₂+…≤b₁, x≥0
Slack variable  convert  ax + by ≤ c  into  ax + by + s = c  (s ≥ 0)
Worked example
Graphical method — product mix A firm makes Product A (profit Rs 40) and Product B (profit Rs 30). A needs 3 machine-hrs + 1 labour-hr; B needs 2 machine-hrs + 1 labour-hr. Available: 60 machine-hrs, 25 labour-hrs.
Maximise Z = 40x + 30y subject to 3x+2y≤60, x+y≤25.
Corner points: (0,0)→0, (20,0)→800, (0,25)→750, and the intersection (10, 15) → Z = Rs 850 — the best plan: 10 units of A and 15 units of B per day.

Unit 9 · Linear Algebra & Applications
Matrices, Determinants, Cramer's Rule, Gauss Elimination & Input-Output Analysis
+

Behind every spreadsheet model juggling several products, ingredients, or economic sectors at once, a matrix is quietly doing the heavy lifting.

Key ideas
  • Types of matrices — row, column, square, diagonal, identity and zero matrices.
  • Matrix operations — addition, subtraction, scalar multiplication, matrix multiplication and the transpose.
  • Determinants, minors & cofactors — a single number that reveals whether a matrix is singular (no inverse) or non-singular (invertible).
  • Adjoint & inverse matrix — the tools for "dividing" by a matrix.
  • Solving systems of equations: Cramer's rule (via determinants), the inverse-matrix method, and Gauss elimination / Gauss-Jordan (via row operations) — each suited to different problem sizes.
  • Input-output (Leontief) analysis: a technology-coefficient matrix models how each economic sector depends on every other; the Hawkins-Simon condition checks that the whole system can actually produce enough to meet demand.
Core formulas
Cramer's rule  xᵢ = det(Aᵢ) / det(A)   ·  Inverse  A⁻¹ = adj(A) / det(A)
Leontief model  X = (I − A)⁻¹ D   ·  viable only if every leading principal minor of (I − A) is positive
Worked example
Cramer's rule Solve 2x + 3y = 8 and x − 2y = −3.
D = −7, Dx = −7, Dy = −14  →  x = 1, y = 2 (check: 2(1)+3(2)=8 ✓)

Input-output (2 sectors) Technology matrix A = [[0.2, 0.3], [0.4, 0.1]], final demand D = [100, 60].
det(I−A) = 0.6 > 0 → Hawkins-Simon condition satisfied. Gross output X₁ ≈ 180, X₂ ≈ 147.

Unit 10 · Functions of Several Variables
Partial Differentiation, Utility, Production & Lagrange Multipliers
+

Real firms rarely decide with just one variable — output depends on labour and capital, and utility depends on many goods bought together.

Key ideas
  • Functions of two or more variables: z = f(x, y), such as a utility function U(x, y) or a production function Q(L, K).
  • Partial differentiation: differentiate with respect to one variable while holding the others constant.
  • Applications: marginal utility, marginal product, and elasticity in a multi-variable setting.
  • Unconstrained optimisation: set every partial derivative to zero, then check second-order conditions.
  • Constrained optimisation with Lagrange multipliers: maximise utility or output subject to a budget or resource limit by introducing a multiplier λ that measures the value of relaxing the constraint by one unit.
Core formulas
Partial derivatives  ∂z/∂x, ∂z/∂y (all other variables held fixed)
Lagrangian  L = f(x,y) + λ [ budget − Pₓx − Pᵧy ]
Optimum condition  MUₓ / Pₓ = MUᵧ / Pᵧ = λ
Worked example
Utility maximisation Maximise U = xy subject to 4x + 2y = 100 (budget Rs 100, prices Rs 4 and Rs 2).
Lagrange conditions give y = 2x. Substituting: 4x + 2(2x) = 100 → x* = 12.5, y* = 25
Maximum utility U* = 12.5 × 25 = 312.5, with λ = 6.25 (the marginal utility of one extra rupee of budget).

Unit 11 · First-Order Differential Equations
Growth Models & the Dynamics of Market Price
+

Prices don't jump to equilibrium instantly — they drift there over time, and a differential equation is the mathematical language of that drift.

Key ideas
  • What a differential equation is: an equation involving a rate of change, classified by order and degree.
  • Separation of variables — the main technique used to solve first-order equations by hand.
  • Growth models: dP/dt = kP describes unlimited exponential growth; variants describe limited growth that levels off.
  • Dynamics of market price: if price adjusts in proportion to the current excess demand, dP/dt = k[Qd(P) − Qs(P)], the price path can converge smoothly to equilibrium — or diverge, depending on the sign of k.
  • Variable-coefficient, exact, and nonlinear first-order equations — further categories with their own solution techniques.
Core formulas
Price adjustment  dP/dt = k [ Qd(P) − Qs(P) ]
Linear solution form  dP/dt + P = A   →  P(t) = A + Ce⁻ᵗ
Worked example
Price converging to equilibrium Qd(P) = 90 − P, Qs(P) = −10 + P. Excess demand = 100 − 2P, and price adjusts at dP/dt = 0.5(100−2P) = 50 − P.
Equilibrium (dP/dt = 0): P = 50. Solving with P(0) = 20: P(t) = 50 − 30e⁻ᵗ
As t → ∞, P(t) → 50 — the price path glides smoothly toward the equilibrium price.

Unit 12 · Dynamic Economic Analysis & Difference Equations
The Cobweb Model, Lagged Keynesian Model & Duopoly Price Adjustment
+

When a farmer plans next season's crop based on this season's price, we've left continuous time behind — welcome to the world of difference equations.

Key ideas
  • Difference equations relate a variable's value this period (Pₜ) to its value in a previous period (Pₜ₋₁), rather than to a continuous rate of change.
  • Solving first-order difference equations — both homogeneous and non-homogeneous cases.
  • The Cobweb model: demand reacts to today's price, but supply is decided using last period's price — producing a price path that spirals toward equilibrium, spirals away from it, or oscillates forever, depending on the relative steepness of demand and supply.
  • The lagged Keynesian macroeconomic model: this period's income responds to last period's spending decisions.
  • Duopoly price adjustment: two competing firms each set price/output in reaction to the other's previous move.
Core formulas
Cobweb recurrence  Pₜ = A − r·Pₜ₋₁,  where r = (slope of supply) / (slope of demand)
Stability rule  |r| < 1 → converges (damped oscillation)  ·  |r| > 1 → diverges
Worked example
Cobweb model — convergent case Qdₜ = 100 − 3Pₜ  ·  Qsₜ = −20 + 2Pₜ₋₁
Equilibrium: 100−3Pe = −20+2Pe → Pe = 24, Qe = 28. Recurrence: Pₜ = 40 − (2/3)Pₜ₋₁, so r = 2/3 < 1.
Starting at P₀ = 18: P₁=28, P₂=21.3, P₃=25.8, P₄=22.8 — the price oscillates with shrinking swings, converging toward Rs 24.
